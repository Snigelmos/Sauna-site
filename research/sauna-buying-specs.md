## Sauna types compared (infrared vs traditional electric vs wood)
Sources: Sauna Guide, Commonplace, Sun Home, RecoveryStack, Healing Sauna.

| Factor | Traditional (electric/wood) | Infrared |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Operating temp | 150-195F (some to 230F) | 110-150F |
| How it heats | Heats the AIR (and rocks); body from outside in | Radiant light heats the BODY directly |
| Humidity / loyly | Yes - water on rocks, 10-60% RH | None (dry) |
| Heat-up time | 30-45 min | 10-20 min |
| Session length | 15-20 min (with cool-down cycles) | 30-45 min |
| Power draw | 4.5-9 kW | 1.5-3 kW (panels) |
| Electrical | 240V dedicated circuit, electrician | Usually 120V standard outlet, plug-and-play |
| Install cost | $1,000-$3,000+ | $0-$500 |
| New cost (2026) | $3,000-$14,000+ | $1,500-$6,000 |
| Monthly operating | $15-$30 | $5-$15 |
| Sweat character | High volume, fast onset | Lower volume, "deeper" feeling |
| Best for | Heat/steam lovers, social, outdoor, max evidence | Daily indoor use, recovery, easy install, renters |
| Ventilation | Needed (esp. with steam) | Minimal |

Key honest point (RecoveryStack): the cardiovascular longevity research is overwhelmingly on TRADITIONAL Finnish saunas. Infrared has thinner evidence but real benefits, and you'll actually use it daily without a 240V install. Pick on lifestyle, not health-claim hype.

Wood-burning notes: best for outdoor/detached; needs chimney, heat shielding, fire-rated clearances; heat-up 60-90 min; superior loyly per enthusiasts; off-grid capable; more maintenance (15-30 hrs/yr vs 2-4 for electric).

## EMF (the "low EMF" buyer concern)
- Cheap carbon-panel infrared units can emit measurable AC magnetic fields, sometimes ~30-80 mG at the panel surface.
- IARC classifies extremely-low-frequency magnetic fields as Group 2B (possible carcinogen) on weak epidemiological signals.
- Premium brands (Sun Home, Clearlight, Sunlighten) market low-EMF panels, typically 1-5 mG at body distance.
- Buyer rule: want under ~3 mG at body distance; demand third-party (accredited-lab) EMF testing covering BOTH magnetic and electric fields, measured where the body sits. Be skeptical of vague "low EMF" labels. A Trifield TF2 meter (~$175) lets buyers verify.
- Practical: don't press your back directly against panels for a full 45-min daily session. Concern is "worth thinking about, not panicking about."

## Sizing (interior) - person capacity is inflated
Rules: 20 in bench length per seated adult; ~70 in to lie flat; aim for 7 ft ceiling for heat stratification. Reduce advertised "person" capacity by ~25-33% for real comfort.
Cubic feet = L x W x H. Heater ~1 kW per 45-50 cu ft; +15-25% for glass/poor insulation/cold; +~6 cu ft per sq ft of glass; +25-50% effective volume for uninsulated/log/concrete.

| Interior | Floor | Volume (7ft) | Heater | Heat-up | Realistic capacity |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 4x6 ft | 24 sq ft | 168 cu ft | 4.5-6 kW | 25-35 min | 1-2 |
| 5x7 ft | 35 sq ft | 245 cu ft | 6-8 kW | 30-45 min | 2-3 |
| 6x8 ft | 48 sq ft | 336 cu ft | 8-9 kW | 40-55 min | 3-4 |
| 7x9 ft | 63 sq ft | 441 cu ft | 9-12 kW | 45-65 min | 4-6 |

Infrared sizing is by panel count/occupants: 1-person ~300-600W (1-2 panels); 2-person ~1,200-1,800W (3-4 panels); 3-4 person ~2,400-3,200W (6-8 panels).

## Wood / materials
Source: ThermalFinn wood table, Sweat Decks.

| Species | Thermal conductivity (W/mK) | Cost ($/board ft) | Best use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Aspen | 0.10 | $3-5 | Benches (cool to touch) |
| Abachi | 0.09 | $8-14 | Premium benches |
| Western Red Cedar | 0.11 | $6-10 | Walls/ceiling/benches; rot-resistant, aroma, 20-30 yr |
| Clear cedar | - | ~+30% vs cedar | Premium appearance, no knots |
| Nordic Spruce | 0.12 | $3-5 | Walls/ceiling (budget) |
| Thermally Modified Spruce/Thermowood | 0.10-0.11 | $7-12 | Walls/ceiling/benches (premium, very stable, outdoor) |
| Redwood | - | premium, hard to source | Walls/ceiling |
| Hemlock | - | low | Entry-tier kits, infrared (scentless, softer) |

Best-value strategy: aspen/abachi on BENCH surfaces (lowest conductivity = won't burn bare skin), cedar or spruce on walls/ceiling. NEVER use resinous/knotty pine on bench surfaces - resin liquefies at sauna temps and creates sticky, burning spots. Wood cost for a 5x7 sauna: ~$660-1,100 (Nordic spruce) to $1,540-2,640 (thermowood); mixed approach ~$660-1,100 best value.

## Build-quality tiers (kits)
- Budget ($2,500-): big-box hemlock/generic, 4.5-6 kW, minimal support.
- Mid-premium ($6,000-$14,000): cedar/redwood/thermowood shell, 6-9 kW with proper stone mass, full-thickness clear cedar/aspen benches, real gasketing, install manuals + phone support; lasts 20+ years.
